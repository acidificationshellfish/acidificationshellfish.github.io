> Effect on Predators

Predator-prey populations are correlated, as such the declining population of shellfish would ripple through the trophic levels. However, that is not always for granted as low pH acts on predators as well as the mollusks.

The study on blue mussels and common starfish (Asterias rubens) found that blue mussels increased in shell length as an effect of low pH while common starfish decreased in population and average wet mass. An increase in shellfish population in this study cannot be solely attributed to pH, as the interaction with the native predator has shifted in favor of the blue mussel. However, it is worth noting that an increase in shell size between generations can be a result of maternal investment as a result of low pH.

In contrast, the study on Olympia oysters and the invasive Atlantic oyster drill snail (Urosalpinx cinerea) found that the Olympia oysters decreased in shell size between generations. The snail drilled more oysters in environments with higher CO2 due to the smaller size of the oysters. Smaller oysters take less time overall to drill and offer less energy per consumption, which leads drill snails to consume more oysters. The researcher notes that invasive species see less effects from ocean acidification overall compared to native species.

> Chemical Process of Ocean Acidification

The ocean is a natural carbon sink, and in the contemporary time with rising CO2 levels in the atmosphere, ambient CO2 levels rise in ocean waters. CO2 and H2O bond to form carbonic acid, H2CO3, which dissociates into ions. The bicarbonate ion, HCO3-, then further dissociates into H+ and the carbonate ion, CO32-.

Free H+ ions lower the pH of oceanic waters, hence causing them to become more acidic. Overall, ocean pH has been at 8.2 pH due to the carbonate buffer system, though in recent times it has lowered to 8.1 pH. Many ocean creatures have high sensitivity to pH, and even subtle changes affect populations.

H2CO3 is considered unstable, which is why it dissociates. The ions that form from it, H+, HCO3-, and CO32-, create the carbonate buffer system. When H+ ions increase in the setting, CO32- ions bond to form bicarbonate and increase pH. The carbonate buffer system exists not only in ocean water, but to different extents in the blood of animals, which correlates with their tolerance to acids.

Of all the ions represented in this buffer cycle, CO32- is particularly important to calcifiers.
